Part I — The Artist
Dan Katz, Illustrator
Everything here was assembled by AI from your public portfolio, Reedsy profile, and web presence — to show what's possible when machine intelligence meets artistic depth.
Dan Katz is an Atlanta-based oil painter and illustrator whose work lives at the intersection of painterly realism and narrative imagination. Trained at Columbus College of Art & Design (2005–2009) and sharpened through nearly a decade of Illustration Master Class attendance (2009–2018) and Smart Art School advanced studies, Dan brings a rare combination: classical oil technique applied to speculative and fantastical subjects.
His work doesn't look like AI-generated illustration. That's the point. In an era flooding with synthetic imagery, Dan's paintings carry the texture of physical media, the warmth of impasto brushwork, and the intentionality of traditional composition.
Based in Atlanta, freelancing since 2022 after 7+ years as a graphic designer at Kason Industries. Currently open for commissions and contracts through Reedsy and direct outreach.

Translation: Dan doesn't chase photorealism. He leaves the brush visible. Every stroke is a conscious decision — not rendered, authored. Compositions are structured with graphic intent. This is illustration in the truest sense.
"He has always appreciated both the serious and the fun"
Translation: The dragon-commuting-to-work piece exists in the same portfolio as Monte Cristo. Most illustrators pick a lane. Dan's range means he can match any author's tone. That's a commercial superpower for book illustration.

Translation: Dan's oil handling is unusual — thin in places, creating translucency typically associated with watercolor. This creates atmospheric depth that digital painting struggles to match. A technical signature.

Translation: Not just a painter — a collaborator. Dan reads the manuscript, talks to the author, and makes visual decisions that serve the story. This is where AI struggles most.

Part II — Working Together
Where AI Fits Your Workflow
Not selling you AI. Showing you where it removes friction from work you're already doing.

The honest premise

AI can generate images. You know this. You've probably seen it flood your market with mediocre fantasy art. Here's what AI cannot do:

It can't load a brush with cadmium yellow and drag it through titanium white with the pressure that comes from painting the same stroke ten thousand times. It can't read a manuscript and feel which moment is the cover. It can't sit with a client and hear the difference between what they say they want and what they actually need.

Your hands, your eye, your judgment — those are the product. AI is useful for everything around that: finding clients, managing content, analyzing markets, handling the business of being an artist so you can focus on the art of being an artist.
